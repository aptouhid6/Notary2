What’s the role of the notary when it comes to the signing of those important documents?

A notary public is an official of integrity appointed by the state government to serve the public as an impartial witness to prevent fraud related to the signing of important documents. They must notarize documents following the laws where they got their commission. In our case, Texas.

When a document is notarized, it only certifies that the signature on a document is genuine, a document was signed on a specific date or a copy is an accurate reproduction of the original. It does not make the document legal or truthful.

In order to prevent fraud, a notary must properly identify the signer following their state laws guidance. They must also scan the signer for willingness and awareness (awareness of what they’re signing and state of mind in general). Some documents might even require the notary to put the signer under an oath, declaring under penalty of perjury that the information contained in the document is true and correct. That’s why the personal appearance of the signer in front of the notary is a must!

One of the notary’s most important job is to properly identify the signer. If the signer is unknown to the notary, then an acceptable ID is required. Acceptable IDs for notarization purposes in the state of Texas is an unexpired card or other document issued by the U.S federal government or any U.S state government that contains the photograph and signature of the signer. Proper IDs include unexpired:

 

  • U.S. State driver’s licenses
  • U.S. nondriver’s ID cards
  • U.S. Passports
  • U.S. military ID cards
  • Permanent Resident Cards issued by USCIS (Green cards)
  • Foreign Passport: Only for residential real estate transaction. Examples of documents would be deeds of trust, affidavits supporting refinance or purchase transactions, and powers of attorney for the purpose of conveying real property or for executing documents related to real property loan transactions.
  • Credible Witness: In the absence of acceptable IDs, a credible identifying witness may be used. The credible witness must have acceptable ID, must be personally known to the notary and know the signer long enough to take an oath, declaring under penalty of perjury that the signer is who they say they are.
